Listeriosis is a disease caused by the microscopic bacteria Listeria monocytogenes. While Listeria monocytogenes can be found worldwide in the soil, the disease listeriosis is typically transmitted through infected food. Food can become infected at the farm or during processing and because of the resilient nature of the bacteria, Listeria monocytogenes can grow even at typical refrigeration temperatures of 4˚ C.

While listeriosis is fairly rare, in pregnant women listeriosis is 12-20 times more prevalent than in the general population, and the incidence in newborn babies is estimated at a rate of 8.6 per 100,000 live births. While symptoms in pregnant women are mild, Listeria monocytogenes can travel through the placenta from the mother to the unborn child and can result in fetal death in 20-30% of cases, as well as preterm delivery and infection in newborns.

In pregnant women symptoms are typically very mild and in some cases infected people may not exhibit symptoms at all. Typical symptoms in pregnant woman are a fever or other mild flu-like symptoms, but listeriosis can frequently cause fetal death, preterm delivery or infection of newborns. In infected newborns, symptoms include respiratory distress, fever, rash, jaundice, or lethargy.

If you suspect you have come in contact with Listeria monocytogenes, please contact your doctor or healthcare provider immediately. Blood cultures can be performed to determine if you have contracted listeriosis. If you have contracted listeriosis, you may recieve treatment with antibiotics.

While you are pregnant, there are several steps you can take to protect yourself from Listeria monocytogenes.

  • Proper kitchen hygiene- avoid cross contamination between raw meats and other foods, clean and disinfect cutting boards and working surfaces, and cook meats to an appropriate internal temperature
  • Avoid foods that are known to harbour Listeria monocytogenes, or are eaten without being cooked. This includes ready to eat deli meats, including lunch meats,hotdogs or pate, smoked seafood, as well as soft cheese made with unpasteurized milk such as brie or Mexican soft cheese. Unpasteurized milk can also be a source of listeriosis. If you do eat these foods, they should be heated to an appropriate internal temperature. You can check the temperature of your food with a meat thermometer.
